My Bad Cat Mitten

Mitten the "Wonder Why We Kept Him" Cat Skip to Tip Table

I have a very bad cat named Mitten. I got him from a woman named Debby, who packed a bunch her own problems into a cardboard box, dumped them onto the floor at work, and made one of them mine. Even after all these years (14, I think), I can't believe she gave me such a bad cat.

The Early Years

Dude Looks Like a Lady

The first and most irrational problem I have with Mitten is that he was supposed to be a girl. I just prefer female pets. Debby promised me he was a she. I even held up one of his calico sisters and said, "I know this is a girl. All calicos are girls. How come this one doesn't look the same?" But she swore, and I believed her, and I brought home Mitten, whom I planned on naming Ruby. Hmf!

That's How He Never Got His Name

The vet explained to me that the "difference" I had seen was a descended testicle and though he'd seen a lot of things in his career, he'd never seen a female kitty with one of those. Ruby needed a name change.

Rudy/Rudolph seemed like an obvious choice. It was December after all. It wasn't, and neither was Hugo. We had another cat named Kitty, so we couldn't fall back on that old copout, and for months just called him Kitten. I came home late from work to see him up to something bad and cried, "Kitten McMitten." And the last part stuck for some reason.

But it's a dumb name and I do feel a little guilty wondering whether that dumb name contributed to his badness. I mean, there are countless articles on the influence of a name on business and relationship success. Why not pet success? Did I doom the poor guy? Did not giving him a real name make him feel unloved? Are these questions signs that I should have had children?

Early Signs

Honestly, though, I probably should have known by the way he fought the entire car ride home to be let out of the box, poking his tiny white paw out of the top, razor-sharp claws tearing at my hands as I tried to hold the flaps. I probably should have known.

Or the way he—on his first night with us—climbed down almost immediately from the cat tree I'd placed him on for safety and hissed his way past the two dogs (70 and 45 pounds) and other cat that were his new siblings. He had to get to the kitchen, which contained his two favorite things, Mommy and food (order unknown).

Or the way he, the very next morning, managed to bully a cheese-covered pill from a 70-pound dog who lived for food and wind up in the hospital for the first of what has gone on to be several necessary medical iterventions for this little knucklehead.

The Mr. Pee-body Years

Years of medical bills for a gaping wound on his side and an injured paw were nothing compared to when he got a urinary tract infection and began peeing on whatever. My couch, my bed, workmen's dropcloths, nothing was spared. He soaked the carpet in the dining room so badly that when we replaced the carpeting, we also had to replace the baseboard and the lower 6 inches of wall board. Long after the UTI was gone and he was on extremely expensive maintenance food to prevent a recurrence, he kept peeing, not always but often enough.

These were definitely the worst of times. There simply is only so much cat pee one can put up with. Yelling and intimidation made things worse. We wanted to give him away but couldn't. We talked about having him put down but there was no way. We honestly just dealt with it until he (mostly) outgrew it.

After all, he wasn't all bad. Who is? He once ran from five rooms away out the door to chase away a cat that was threatening his sister, who had neither his size nor his moxie. He was and always has been my biggest cuddler. With all the pain I've felt when I absolutely knew I had to put a pet down, there was no way I coud do it for behavior reasons. So we loved and perservered and cleaned until he got over it (for the most part).

A Changed Cat

Today Mitten is Mr. Mellow, but he's still a pretty strange cat. We blame the dog's medicine for messing with his brain. If it storms in the middle of the night, you have to take him to the litter box or he may pee on the bed (thank goodness for waterproof matress pads). He likes to have his claws trimmed, even asks for it. He rarely grooms himself and hates to be brushed. He sometimes chews wood, including his favorite toy, the block of wood shown in the photo.

All of Mitten's siblings are gone. He's the end of an era but seems to enjoy his one-on-one time bouncing between laps. He was very close to his older sister, Kitty, later renamed Big Kitty. Her new name made sense when Mitten was little, and after he outweighed her by 10 pounds, it was ironic and matched her personality.

I have no real answer for why we kept Mitten through the many, many bad times, far too numerous to outline here. I guess because deep down he is the Heathcliff in us all, the flawed tragic character with just enough redeemable qualities to make you root for him. Also, life with him is just plain more interesting. Oh, and he's a hell of a mouser.
